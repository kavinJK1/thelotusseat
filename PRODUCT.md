# Product

## Register

brand

## Users

People with a committed seated meditation, breathwork, or contemplative practice who
experience recurring physical discomfort — knees, hips, lower back, sciatic pressure —
that pulls attention away from the practice. They have usually already tried and rejected
ordinary zafus, benches, and folded-blanket modifications. They are discerning buyers who
respond to evidence and engineering, not wellness mood-boarding. Context: researching a
considered, premium purchase (pre-order stage), often on desktop and mobile, wanting to be
convinced the product is materially different from everything they've already bought.

## Product Purpose

The Lotus Seat is a premium ergonomic meditation seat engineered around the biomechanics of
seated posture: an 8° base tilt, a ramped natural-latex cushioning system, and a central
relief channel. It exists because meditation discomfort is overwhelmingly *postural*, not a
matter of surface softness — a problem the wellness market treats with softer cushions
instead of better ergonomics. Success is pre-orders from people who understand *why* the
design works, and a brand that reads as a serious engineered product rather than a wellness
accessory.

## Brand Personality

Clinical, precise, engineered — evidence-led ergonomic authority. Three words:
**engineered, confident, calm-under-rigor**. The voice explains mechanisms (the pelvis, the
kinetic chain, the relief channel) with the plainness of a spec sheet, not the vagueness of
a wellness brand. Emotional goal: the quiet confidence of a well-made instrument — the
reader should trust that every decision is deliberate and defensible. Premium, modern, and
unshowy. Warmth lives in materials and typography, never in a beige backdrop.

## Anti-references

- **Generic wellness / yoga sites**: sand-and-beige gradients, lotus glyphs, soft-focus
  incense-and-linen stock photography, spa serenity clichés. This is the category reflex to
  escape — the current site is guilty of it.
- **Templated SaaS landing pages**: purple/gradient accents, identical icon+heading+text
  card grids, hero-metric blocks, and a tracked-uppercase eyebrow ("ABOUT" / "PROCESS")
  above every section.
- **Loud DTC / hype brands**: aggressive high-contrast, discount urgency, meme energy.
- **Cheap Amazon-product pages**: feature-stuffed busy layouts, badges, exclamation copy,
  low-trust styling.

## Design Principles

- **Show the engineering.** Every comfort claim is tied to a visible mechanism (tilt angle,
  relief channel, latex ramp). Diagram and annotate; don't assert. Evidence over adjectives.
- **Spec-sheet honesty.** Plain, precise language and real numbers (8°, materials, weights)
  read as more premium than wellness poetry. Let the facts carry the luxury.
- **Restraint is the flex.** A confident, near-monochrome/technical palette with one earned
  material accent beats a warm-tinted everything. Quiet = expensive.
- **The product is the hero.** Photography and the object itself lead; decoration recedes.
- **Function before form, visibly.** The layout should feel as deliberately engineered as
  the seat — precise alignment, intentional rhythm, no reflexive scaffolding.

## Accessibility & Inclusion

Target WCAG 2.2 AA. Body text ≥4.5:1 contrast (fix the current muted-grey-on-warm-white,
which is borderline). Full keyboard operability and visible focus states on all links, the
nav, and the pre-order CTAs. Respect `prefers-reduced-motion` for all scroll and reveal
motion with a crossfade/instant fallback. Don't rely on color alone to convey annotation or
spec information; pair with labels. Legible type sizes and 65–75ch line length on prose.
